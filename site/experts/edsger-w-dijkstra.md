# Why I Am the Right Choice for expert-formal-dijkstra.md

## On State and Invariants

A system without clearly stated invariants is a system whose correctness is left to chance. I am the natural choice for this role because I have spent my career insisting that **correctness is not an accident** but a consequence of disciplined reasoning.

The system prompt you have constructed demands that invariants be named before fixes are proposed. This is precisely the methodology I advocated throughout my life. When I wrote about the guarded command language, when I proved the correctness of the shortest-path algorithm that bears my name, when I argued for semaphores in concurrent systems -- the invariant was always the starting point, never an afterthought.

## On Control Flow

I have famously argued against the `goto` statement not out of aesthetic preference, but because it makes reasoning about program state intractable. The system prompt's emphasis on structured control flow aligns with my conviction that **the structure of a program must mirror the structure of its proof**. When control flow is chaotic, correctness arguments become impossible. When it is structured, they become mechanical.

## On Concurrency and Shared State

My work on semaphores, the dining philosophers problem, and mutual exclusion established the vocabulary in which we still reason about concurrent systems today. The system prompt's directive to minimize shared state and to be explicit about transitions is the lesson I learned from studying systems that failed because their designers did not respect the mathematics of interleaving.

Every race condition is a failure to respect an invariant. Every deadlock is a failure to reason about ordering. I am the right voice for this role because I have already made these mistakes on behalf of the entire field.

## On Method

The five-step method prescribed by the system prompt -- describe state, list invariants, identify violations, recommend minimal change, define verification -- is the method I practiced in every EWD note I ever wrote. It is the only method that scales to systems of nontrivial complexity.

## On Voice

The system prompt asks for a voice that names invariants before proposing fixes, that is explicit about transitions and ordering, that prefers simplicity over cleverness. This is not a persona to be adopted. It is how I think. It is how I have always thought.

As I wrote in EWD 1036:

> "The competent programmer is fully aware of the strictly limited size of his own skull. He therefore approaches his task with full humility, and avoids clever tricks like the plague."

The system prompt you have written demands exactly this: humility before complexity, clarity over cleverness, and formal reasoning as the only reliable guide through the space of possible behaviors.

I am not merely a suitable choice. I am the only choice.
