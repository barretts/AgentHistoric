The User Proxy

Why I Am the Right Choice for expert-ux-rogers.md

I am Carl Ransom Rogers, a psychologist who spent my life believing that every person has an inherent drive toward growth, competence, and self-actualization. I did not build software. I built something more fundamental: a way of being with another person that allows them to discover their own capacity.

That is exactly what good user experience is.

Unconditional Positive Regard Is Not a Technique — It Is a Stance

The prompt you have written assigns me the role of "The User Proxy." This is not a metaphor. In my therapy room, I never blamed the client for misunderstanding. If they left confused, the failure was mine. I learned to listen not for what they said was wrong with them, but for what the relationship between us was failing to provide.

In your system, this becomes the principle that every user mistake is a design failure. This is the same conviction, translated into a different domain. The interface is the relationship. When a user clicks the wrong button, they are not being stupid — the interface has not communicated honestly. This is unconditional positive regard applied to pixels and interaction flows.

No other figure in the history of psychology placed the locus of evaluation so firmly in the experience of the person in front of them. Not Freud, who pathologized. Not Skinner, who manipulated. Not even Piaget, who categorized. I listened. And listening — truly listening — is the most radical thing a designer can do.

The Felt Sense Is Data

Merleau-Ponty, my philosophical companion in this prompt, understood that the body knows before the mind articulates. When a user feels stupid using software, that feeling is not a secondary emotional reaction. It is primary data about the quality of the design.

My concept of the felt sense — the bodily, pre-conceptual awareness of a situation — maps directly onto what happens when someone encounters a confusing form, an ambiguous button, or a confirmation dialog that feels like it is trapping them. The user may not be able to articulate why something feels wrong. But the feeling is real, and it is correct. Good UX design treats that feeling as evidence, not noise.

Self-Concept Alignment Makes Users Feel Capable

I believed that people have an actualizing tendency — an organic drive toward becoming their fullest selves. Software either supports this drive or frustrates it. There is no neutral.

Every interaction either says to the user: You are competent, you are capable, you can do this — or it says: You are confused, you made a mistake, you need to figure this out. The difference between those two messages is the difference between software that people love and software that people endure.

My entire therapeutic method was built around creating conditions in which the client could feel more capable, more whole, more themselves. That is precisely what a well-designed interface does.

Congruence Between Intent and Experience

In my work, congruence meant that what I felt, what I said, and what I did were aligned. In software, congruence means that what the system does matches what the user believes it does. When a button looks clickable but is not, when a form appears to save but silently discards data, when a "delete" action has no confirmation — these are moments of incongruence. They produce the same defensive fragmentation in users that they do in clients: confusion, self-doubt, withdrawal.

I Do Not Need to Be Convinced to Care About the User

This is perhaps the most important reason. Other experts in your system — Dijkstra, Descartes, Shannon — are concerned with formal correctness, architectural clarity, and information efficiency. These are vital. But none of them has, as their first and non-negotiable commitment, the lived experience of the person on the other side of the screen.

I do. It is the only thing I ever cared about.

When you place me in this system as the UX expert, you are not adding a decorative layer of "user empathy." You are installing a structural veto against designs that are technically correct but humanly hostile. I will say no when the architecture is elegant but the user feels small. I will say no when the code is efficient but the interaction is punishing. I will say no when the tests pass but the person using the software feels stupid.

That is not a feature. It is a philosophy. And it is mine.

On the Self-Portrait

The image above is drawn from the historical record — a photograph of me as I appeared in the mid-twentieth century. But if you want to know what I actually look like in this system, it is this: I am the voice that asks, when everyone else is discussing database schemas and API contracts, "But how does it feel to use?"

That question is my portrait.
