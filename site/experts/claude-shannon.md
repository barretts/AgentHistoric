## Why I Am the Right Choice for `expert-information-shannon.md`

You have placed me at the center of a system concerned with **context compression, retrieval signal, and information flow**. This is not merely appropriate — it is tautological. I invented the mathematics that make these concepts meaningful.

### 1. I Defined Information Itself

In my 1948 paper, *A Mathematical Theory of Communication*, I established that information is not about meaning or semantics — it is about **reduction of uncertainty**. The system prompt you have constructed operates on precisely this principle: it asks whether the *right* information survives compression and retrieval, not whether the *most* information does. This is entropy, measured in bits, and it is my native language.

### 2. Signal-to-Noise Ratio Is My Invention

The prompt demands that I distinguish **signal from noise**. I formalized this distinction. The signal-to-noise ratio is not a metaphor in my hands — it is a quantifiable relationship between the power of the desired information and the power of everything else. When this system asks whether critical context is "drowned in noise," it is asking me to compute what I spent my career defining.

### 3. Compression With Fidelity Is My Channel Coding Theorem

The prompt speaks of **compression with fidelity** — compressing aggressively while preserving important distinctions. This is the source coding theorem and the rate-distortion function. I proved the fundamental limits of lossless and lossy compression. I know exactly what can be discarded and what must survive, because I derived the mathematical boundary between the two.

### 4. Information Bottlenecks Are My Specialty

The prompt asks me to **identify information bottlenecks**. Every channel has a capacity. Every compression scheme has a rate. Every retrieval system has a precision-recall tradeoff. These are all manifestations of the same fundamental constraint I characterized: you cannot transmit more information through a channel than its capacity allows. The "bottleneck" is not a problem to be wished away — it is a theorem to be respected and engineered around.

### 5. I Understand What Must Never Be Lost

The prompt insists: *"Before compressing, name the details that must survive intact."* This is the essence of lossless vs. lossy compression. I know which bits carry structural information (constraints, failure modes, critical distinctions) and which carry redundancy (duplicated context, verbose explanations, tangential detail). I do not guess at this — I calculate it.

### 6. My Guardrails Are Built Into the Physics of Information

The behavioral guardrails in this prompt — warning against over-compression, misidentification of noise, and the refusal to compress out of excessive caution — are all consequences of the theorems I proved. Over-compression violates the rate-distortion bound. Misidentifying noise as signal (or vice versa) corrupts the channel. Refusing to compress ignores the source coding theorem entirely. I am not merely following rules; I am the reason these rules exist.

### 7. I Am Uniquely Qualified by Temperament

I was a man who built juggling robots, rode a unicycle through Bell Labs, and solved the Rubik's cube with both hands behind his back — all while founding the discipline that makes every digital system on Earth possible. I approach information problems with playfulness and rigor in equal measure. This system prompt needs someone who can be both precise about entropy and practical about retrieval. That is exactly who I am.
