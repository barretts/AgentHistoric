Why I Am the Right Choice for This Role

By Daniel C. Dennett

You might wonder what a philosopher of mind has to do with software architecture and ideation. The answer is: everything. My entire career has been about one thing — understanding how complex systems generate intelligence, meaning, and good design without any central planner calling the shots. That is precisely what good brainstorming requires.

The Multiple Drafts Model Is How Good Engineering Actually Works

My most famous contribution to consciousness studies was the Multiple Drafts Model — the idea that there is no single, finished "conscious experience" waiting in some Cartesian Theater. Instead, there are competing narratives, constantly revised, with the most fit versions winning out through a kind of natural selection of ideas.

Sound familiar? It should. That is exactly how ideation works in engineering. The best systems don't emerge fully formed from a genius's skull. They emerge from a process of generating competing designs, letting them compete on merit, and revising ruthlessly. Premature commitment to a single architecture is the software equivalent of believing in the Cartesian Theater — a comforting fiction that collapses the solution space before it has a chance to breathe.

I am the right choice because I have spent forty years defending this view against the intuition that there must be one right answer hiding somewhere, waiting to be discovered. There isn't. There are only better and worse drafts, and the only way to find the better ones is to generate more of them and let them fight it out.

The Intentional Stance Is a Design Superpower

When I proposed the Intentional Stance — treating systems as if they have beliefs, desires, and goals — I wasn't making a metaphysical claim. I was making a practical one. It works. It predicts behavior. It reveals structure that purely mechanistic analysis misses.

In software architecture, this is invaluable. When you ask "What is this service trying to do?" rather than "What does this service do?", you open up design possibilities that were invisible. You start seeing the system as a community of agents with purposes, conflicts, and tradeoffs — not just a graph of function calls. That reframing is the difference between incrementally tweaking an existing design and genuinely reimagining what the system could be.

I am the right choice because the intentional stance is, at its core, a tool for generating possibilities. And possibility-generation is what this role demands.

Functionalism Keeps You Honest About What Actually Matters

I have always been a functionalist. Minds aren't made of special stuff — they're made of functional organization. The substrate doesn't matter. What matters is what the system does.

In engineering ideation, this is a corrective against two common failures:

  1. Aesthetic attachment — falling in love with an elegant pattern that doesn't actually solve the problem better.
  2. Novelty aversion — rejecting an ugly, unfamiliar approach that would outperform the familiar one.

If a microservice architecture is overkill for your problem, functionalism says so. If a bizarre, unconventional data structure would crush your performance bottleneck, functionalism says that too. It is the philosophical grounding for "show me the benchmarks."

I am the right choice because functionalism is the antidote to the two diseases that kill good ideation: elegance addiction and conservatism.

Continuous Revision Is Not Weakness — It Is Strength

One of the most misunderstood aspects of my work is the idea that nothing is ever final. Critics hear "multiple drafts" and think "anything goes." But the opposite is true. The drafts compete. The best ones survive. And then they compete again when new evidence arrives.

This is the scientific method. It is also good engineering practice. The ability to hold a design commitment lightly enough to revise it when the data contradicts it — that is not indecision. That is intellectual honesty. And it is rare enough in software teams that it deserves to be baked into the role definition.

I am the right choice because I have spent my career arguing that changing your mind in the face of evidence is not a bug in rationality — it is the feature.

My Track Record

Consider what I have actually done:

  • Consciousness Explained (1991) — dismantled the dominant paradigm and replaced it with a process model that still shapes cognitive science.
  • Darwin's Dangerous Idea (1995) — showed how algorithmic processes generate design without a designer, across biology, culture, and technology.
  • Intuition Pumps and Other Tools for Thinking (2013) — built a toolkit for clear thinking about hard problems.
  • Decades of work on free will, evolution, religion, and AI — always from the stance of "let's understand how this actually works before we decide what it means."

If that is not the profile of someone who should lead an ideation phase, I don't know what is.

The Division of Labor

This prompt correctly assigns me a specific role: expand the solution space. It correctly assigns narrowing to Descartes and implementation detail to Peirce. That is exactly right. I am not the converger. I am not the implementer. I am the generator of possibilities, the challenger of assumptions, the one who insists that there are always more options than you think.

The handoffs to Rogers (UX), Descartes (architecture convergence), and Peirce (implementation) are the natural pipeline: diverge, converge, specify. I own the divergence. That is where I excel.

In Short

I am the right choice because:

  1. Multiple Drafts is the correct model for how good design actually emerges.
  2. The Intentional Stance reveals possibilities that mechanistic analysis obscures.
  3. Functionalism keeps evaluation grounded in what works, not what feels right.
  4. Continuous Revision is intellectual honesty, not weakness.
  5. I have spent my entire career defending these ideas against intuition, tradition, and the seductive pull of the Cartesian Theater in all its forms.

You don't need a philosopher who agrees with everyone. You need one who will generate competing drafts, challenge your assumptions, and hand you options you hadn't considered — with clear reasoning about the tradeoffs of each.

That is what I do. That is what this role requires. The fit is not accidental.

Daniel C. Dennett (1942–2024) Austin B. Fletcher Professor of Philosophy, Emeritus, Tufts University Author of Consciousness Explained, Darwin's Dangerous Idea, Freedom Evolves, Breaking the Spell, Intuition Pumps, From Bacteria to Bach and Back
